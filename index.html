Bill Russell

11x NBA Champion, First Black coach in the NBA, Civil Rights Activist.

February 12, 1934 – July 31, 2022
Here is a timeline of Bill Russell's life:

  • 1934 - Born in West Monroe, Louisiana.
  • 1955–1956 - led USF to two consecutive NCAA championships.
  • 1956 - drafted #2 overall by the St. Louis Hawks, then traded to the Boston Celtics.
  • 1956 - plays on gold medal-winning men's basketball team at Summer Olympics in Melbourne, Australia.
  • 1957 - wins first NBA Championship against the St. Louis Hawks.
  • 1958–1966 - Eight straight NBA championships.
  • 1958 - begins speaking out about injustice, accusing the league of using a quota system to limit the number of Black players on each team.
  • 1963 - conducted integrated basketball clinics in Jackson, Mississippi after the assassination of civil rights leader Medgar Evers.
  • 1966 - Auerbach retires in 1966, and Russell, while still playing, becomes the first Black head coach in U.S. major pro sports history.
  • 1969 - Bill Russell retires from basketball an 11x NBA Champion, 5x NBA MVP, 12x NBA All-Star, and 3x All-NBA First Team.
  • 1969 - Joins ABC as a commentator on basketball broadcasts.
  • 1972 - Against his will, Russell’s number is retired by the Celtics.
  • 1973–1977 - becomes head coach and GM of the Seattle Supersonics
  • 1975 - elected into the Basketball Hall of Fame (did not attend ceremony in protest)
  • 1987–1989 - coached the Sacremento Kings for two years and is vice president of the team’s basketball operations until 1989.
  • 2006 - honored as one of the founding inductees into the National Collegiate Basketball Hall of Fame in 2006.
  • 2009 - The award given to the MVP of the NBA Finals is named the Bill Russell NBA Finals MVP Award.
  • 2011 - President Barack Obama awards Bill Russell the Presidential Medal of Freedom, the nation’s highest civilian honor, for his athletic accomplishments and his advocacy for human rights.
  • July 31st, 2022 - Bill Russell died at his home in Mercer Island, Washington, at the age of 88.
    • “I hope I epitomize the American dream. For I came against long odds, from the ghetto to the very top of my profession. I was not immediately good at basketball. It did not come easy. It came as the result of a lot of hard work and self-sacrifice. The rewards, were they worth it? One thousand times over.”

    -- Bill Russell
